# Legacy Systems: Why We Can't Let Go

Every codebase contains ghosts — systems that should have been retired years ago but continue to run critical functions. Understanding why we cling to legacy technology reveals as much about human psychology as it does about engineering.

## The Sunk Cost Fallacy

Organizations invest millions in building systems. When those systems age, the investment becomes a psychological anchor:

- **"We spent three years building this"** — past effort justifies continued maintenance
- **"It works, don't touch it"** — functional systems resist replacement
- **"Nobody understands it anymore"** — knowledge loss becomes a preservation argument

The sunk cost fallacy transforms technical debt into an emotional attachment.

## The Fear of Replacement

Replacing a legacy system carries visible risk. Keeping it carries invisible risk. Human cognition is biased toward the visible:

| Action | Visible Risk | Invisible Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Keep legacy | Zero (today) | Security holes, scaling limits |
| Replace | Downtime, bugs, learning curve | Future-proofing, performance |

This asymmetry makes inaction the default choice, even when action is clearly superior.

## The Comfort of Familiarity

Developers form relationships with code. A gnarly old system may be frustrating, but it is a known frustration. A new system introduces unknown frustrations:

1. **Documentation gaps** — the old system is poorly documented, but its behavior is known
2. **Edge cases** — years of bug fixes encoded behavior that new systems must rediscover
3. **Integration complexity** — legacy systems often connect to other legacy systems in undocumented ways

## The Path Forward

Letting go requires reframing the narrative. Legacy systems are not failures — they are evidence of past success. The goal is not to erase them but to honor their function while enabling evolution:

- **Strangulation** — gradually replace components rather than the whole system
- **Encapsulation** — wrap legacy systems in modern interfaces
- **Documentation** — capture implicit knowledge before it is lost

The systems we cannot let go of are often the ones that taught us the most.
